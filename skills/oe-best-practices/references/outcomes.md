# Outcomes

An outcome is a meaningful changed state in customer behavior, product usage, decision quality, learning, or business result that advances the current strategy.

Use these six rules when writing or reviewing outcomes:

1. Hard-separate outcomes from outputs.
   Outputs are what gets shipped or done. Outcomes are what changes because of it: behavior, usage, decision quality, learning, or business result. Do not write a feature, project, task, release, document, artifact, or implementation milestone.

2. Make the actor clear.
   Say who changes: customers, users, practitioners, teams, buyers, agents, maintainers, or another specific actor.

3. Connect customer value and business value.
   The outcome should matter to the customer or user and plausibly matter to the product or business.

4. Make it measurable without making the title a metric.
   Keep the title human-readable. Put proof in the measures, and measure impact rather than activity. Prefer both quantitative and qualitative measures: quantitative evidence shows scale or frequency, while qualitative evidence explains meaning and causality.

5. Give discovery room.
   The outcome should be broad enough that multiple opportunities and solutions could plausibly drive it.

6. Fit the current strategy.
   The outcome should express progress toward the active strategic bet.
